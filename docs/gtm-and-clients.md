## 1. Beachhead Market

### Who is the Ideal First Customer?

**Segment:** Small European constellation operators building VLEO or low-LEO Earth observation missions with satellites in the 50–120 kg class, at 220–300 km altitude, seeking a qualified ITAR-free Hall thruster for continuous drag compensation.

**Specific profile:**
- Satellite mass: 50–120 kg
- Orbit: 220–300 km (VLEO), drag compensation duty cycle >80%
- Power budget: 150–250 W available for propulsion
- Propellant: Krypton-capable (cost driven) or flexible
- Geography: European-headquartered or ESA-programme affiliated
- Timeline: Propulsion procurement decision 2027–2029

**Why this segment first — not geostationary:**

| Factor | VLEO Smallsat Constellation | GEO Satellite |
|---|---|---|
| Cathode life urgency | Critical — continuous duty cycle requires >8,000 hrs | Moderate — station-keeping only, ~5,000 hrs |
| Market gap | No qualified European VLEO HET exists today | Safran PPS-series covers this market |
| Sales cycle | 18–36 months (smaller programmes, faster decisions) | 5–10 years (flagship programmes) |
| Unit economics | 5–50 units per constellation programme | 1–2 units per satellite |
| ẍ 200W match | Ideal — 50–120 kg at 220–300 km is the design point | Underpowered for typical GEO needs |
| ITAR risk | High urgency — Busek ITAR blocks European operators | Safran already covers European GEO demand |
| Competitive moat | No incumbent owns this niche | Safran deeply entrenched |

**Why VLEO constellation operators over standard LEO?**

Standard LEO (400–600 km, e.g. OneWeb at 1,200 km) is already served by Busek BHT-350 (80 units flying on OneWeb) and Safran EPS-X00 entering market. These are contested markets.

VLEO (200–350 km) has no qualified European HET product. Atmospheric drag at 250 km is 50–100× higher than at 550 km. A satellite at 250 km needs 2–3 mN continuous thrust (moderate solar activity), requiring a thruster specifically designed for >8,000 hours rated life — a requirement no current European product meets. This is ẍ's structural opening.

**The beachhead in one sentence:** The first customer is a European small satellite integrator or constellation operator building a VLEO Earth observation programme at 220–280 km, using 50–120 kg satellites, who needs a qualified ITAR-free European thruster by 2028–2029 and cannot wait for Safran EPS-X00 to reach VLEO qualification.

## 3. GTM Phases

### Phase 1 — ESA BIC Incubation, Year 1 (2026–2027)
**Objective:** Establish technical credibility and first institutional relationships. No revenue expected.

**Specific actions:**

1. **ESA OSIP / TDE Open Call submission** — Submit to ESA's Open Space Innovation Platform (OSIP) co-sponsorship call targeting VLEO propulsion. Deadline typically Q2/Q3 annually. Target: €100K–€250K co-funded R&D contract. Max's ESA YGT background is the key warm-contact asset here.

2. **IEPC 2026 / IAC 2026 conference presence** — Submit a technical paper on the Aegis RL magnetic shielding architecture to IEPC (International Electric Propulsion Conference) and present at IAC (International Astronautical Congress). Technical credibility in the propulsion community is a prerequisite for customer conversations with satellite engineers.

3. **ESA BIC network activation** — Leverage the Tehnopol / ESA BIC Estonia network to get introductions to: OHB, NanoAvionics, and CNES. Request a warm introduction from the BIC programme manager to at least 3 of the Tier 1 and Tier 2 targets.

4. **EU Horizon Europe application** — Apply to Horizon Europe EIC Pathfinder or EIC Transition call for VLEO propulsion technology. Budget €150K–€500K range non-dilutive. The ESA BIC backing and ITAR-free European positioning are strong eligibility arguments.

5. **IP filing** — File provisional patent on core dynamic magnetic shielding architecture (Month 3–6 of incubation). Secured IP is a prerequisite for serious customer conversations and for any LOI that might emerge.

6. **University partnership formalisation** — Signed MoU with TalTech or University of Tartu for plasma simulation support and vacuum chamber access. This de-risks the technical programme and adds institutional credibility.

**Year 1 success metric:** At least one ESA co-funded R&D contract or LOI from a named Tier 1 target; patent provisional filed; technical paper submitted to IEPC/IAC.

### Phase 2 — First Customer Engagement & Qualification Campaign, Year 2–3 (2027–2028)

**Objective:** Move from technical credibility to commercial pipeline. First revenue from engineering model deliveries.

**Specific actions:**

1. **Engineering model (EM) build and test** — Complete TRL 4–5 bench demonstration of magnetic shielding with Krypton propellant. Document results to ECSS-E-ST-10-02 (Technology Readiness Levels) standard. This is the gate to customer qualification conversations.

2. **Qualification campaign with anchor customer** — Target one Tier 1 customer (ICEYE or NanoAvionics) to enter a formal qualification programme. Structure as a co-development / early adopter agreement: customer provides satellite interface requirements, ẍ delivers qualified engineering model. Non-recurring engineering (NRE) contract target: €150K–€300K.

3. **ECSS qualification documentation** — Begin drafting ECSS-Q-ST-70 compliant qualification test plan. Involve ESA ESTEC propulsion experts (via BIC relationship) in peer review. This is the credibility signal that primes and large integrators (Airbus, TAS) need to see before committing.

4. **Series A preparation** — Use Year 2 ESA contracts, EM test data, and early customer LOIs to build the Series A investor case. Target: €3–5M Series A Q4 2028–Q1 2029, focused on scaling to flight model (FM) and in-orbit demonstration (IOD).

5. **Hire business development lead** — First non-founder hire. Space industry BD background; strong European institutional network; ideally with procurement experience at a prime contractor.

**Year 2–3 success metric:** Engineering model delivered to at least one customer for independent testing; ECSS test plan approved; Series A term sheet.

### Phase 3 — Series Production & Constellation Deals, Year 4–5 (2029–2030)

**Objective:** First revenue at scale. In-orbit demonstration complete. Constellation framework agreements.

**Specific actions:**

1. **In-orbit demonstration (IOD)** — Partner with a VLEO demonstration mission (ESA Scout, Phi-Sat follow-on, or commercial rideshare via D-Orbit ION) to demonstrate ẍ thruster in orbit at 220–280 km. This is the gate to constellation procurement.

2. **IOD → production qualification** — Convert IOD flight data into a qualified flight model (FM). Submit to ESA for TRL 7 verification. This is the credential required to appear on Airbus or TAS approved supplier lists.

3. **IRIS2 supply chain registration** — Register as a qualified propulsion subsystem supplier with the IRIS2 prime consortium (Airbus + SES + Eutelsat consortium). IRIS2 procurement will extend through 2028–2032; ẍ needs to be on the approved supplier list before procurement milestones lock.

4. **Phase 2 product launch (300–500W)** — Develop and qualify the 300–500W variant targeting the standard LEO market (OneWeb-successor scale). This is the high-volume revenue opportunity; the VLEO product opens the door, the Phase 2 product drives scale economics.

5. **Series B / strategic partnership** — At IOD + first production contract, target Series B (€10–20M) or strategic investment from a satellite prime (OHB, Airbus, TAS) to fund manufacturing scale-up to constellation volumes.

**Year 4–5 success metric:** At least one in-orbit demonstration completed; first framework production contract signed; Phase 2 product development funded.

## 4. Why ESA BIC Specifically

Not Techstars. Not Y Combinator. Not a general deep-tech incubator. ESA BIC.

- **Direct access to ESA's technical infrastructure and procurement network.** ESA BIC Estonia provides access to ESTEC test facilities (including vacuum chambers, propellant handling, plasma diagnostics), ESA programme managers, and — critically — the ESA Technology Transfer and Business Incubation Centre network across 24 sites and 22 ESA member states. For a thruster company that needs validated test data and institutional credibility before any satellite OEM will engage, this infrastructure is irreplaceable. No general tech incubator can substitute for access to the world's largest space propulsion test facility.

- **ECSS qualification pathway and standards support.** Selling hardware to satellite primes requires compliance with ECSS (European Cooperation for Space Standardisation) qualification standards — a multi-year, complex process. ESA BIC provides direct access to the ESA engineers who wrote the standards and the test facilities used to verify against them. This cuts qualification timeline by 12–18 months compared to a standalone startup navigating ECSS independently. For deep-tech hardware, this is the difference between a fundable milestone and a multi-year bottleneck.

- **Institutional credibility with European primes and IRIS2.** Airbus, Thales Alenia Space, and OHB will not engage a space hardware startup that has not been vetted by a credible technical body. ESA BIC endorsement signals that the technology has passed a rigorous scientific and engineering review — it is effectively a first-pass qualification signal for procurement gatekeepers. For the IRIS2 supply chain specifically, ESA BIC membership is a direct credibility credential with the agencies and primes managing the programme.
